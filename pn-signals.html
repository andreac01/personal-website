The PNRelay Project

The PNRelay Project is a multidisciplinary research effort aimed at restoring lost motor and sensory functions in patients with Peripheral Nerve Injuries (PNIs). These injuries disrupt the flow of neural signals between the brain and the body, leading to paralysis, loss of sensation, and chronic pain.

PNRelay introduces a novel concept: an artificial relay that bridges damaged nerves through a combination of bioelectronics, signal processing, and machine learning. The system captures neural signals before the lesion, interprets them using deep learning models, and reinjects the correct signals beyond the injury site, effectively acting as a digital nerve bridge.

My Contribution: Signal Analysis & Classification

My main contribution within the PNRelay Project focuses on the Signal Analysis pipeline, developing algorithms and models to decode ElectroNeuroGraphic (ENG) signals from peripheral nerves. The goal is to interpret these low-amplitude, noisy biological signals in real time, enabling seamless artificial neural relaying.

Preprocessing pipeline for denoising and segmenting ENG signals.

This work integrates signal processing, statistical outlier detection, and deep learning. Below is an overview of the main stages of my contribution.

1. Preprocessing & Noise Removal

ENG signals are extremely faint (below 50 ¬µV) and heavily contaminated by electromyographic (EMG) interference. To extract meaningful information, I developed a multi-stage preprocessing pipeline:

  • Clamping: Removes implausible spikes by limiting the signal range to ¬±30 ¬µV.
  • Filtering: Applies an 8th-order Butterworth band-pass filter (800-2200 Hz) to isolate the ENG frequency band.
  • Downsampling: Reduces sampling rate to 5 kHz, balancing resolution and real-time feasibility.
  • Activity Detection: Segments the data into active 100 ms windows for efficient classification.

These steps significantly improved the signal-to-noise ratio and allowed the neural decoder to focus on meaningful segments.

3. Deep Learning for ENG Classification

I adapted and improved several Convolutional Neural Network architectures to classify ENG signals with high accuracy and low latency:

  • ENGNet: Adaptation of EEGNet with separable and depthwise convolutions for efficient feature extraction.
  • Reverse ENGNet: A novel reordering of the convolutional layers, achieving the same representational power with 20x fewer FLOPs.
  • Inception Time (IT): A multiscale CNN model processing temporal features at different resolutions.
  • Single-Block IT: A lightweight variant reducing parameters by 7.5x with comparable accuracy.

These architectures achieved classification performance exceeding 92% macro F1 score, establishing a new benchmark for efficient neural decoding on ENG datasets.

Results and Key Insights

Performance comparison of four deep learning architectures.

The proposed Reverse ENGNet achieved a 20x computational speedup while maintaining equivalent F1 score to the original ENGNet, making it ideal for future fully implanted real-time systems. Similarly, the Single-Block IT model demonstrated a strong trade-off between performance and simplicity.

Conclusion

My work on the PNRelay Project contributed to developing an end-to-end signal analysis framework , from preprocessing to deep learning-based classification, paving the way toward a functional artificial neural bridge.

These findings are currently being integrated into the next PNRelay prototype, with ongoing research aimed at real-time, low-power implementation suitable for fully implanted devices.

The combination of neuroscience, bioelectronics, and AI at the core of PNRelay represents a promising step toward restoring function and dignity to patients affected by peripheral nerve injuries.
